Advised by Prof. Zhihua Wang at Arizona State University, my doctoral work took an interdisciplinary, data-driven perspective on the hydroclimate system. I treated Earth's climate components as interconnected nodes in a complex network, applying network theory, nonlinear dynamics, and causal inference to unravel how land surface, atmosphere, hydrology, and urbanization truly influence one another — and to detect the critical transitions that shape climate extremes.

Imagine the components of the climate system — land surface, atmosphere, hydrology, urbanization — as nodes in a network, connected by links that encode real physical interactions: energy fluxes, moisture transport, teleconnections. This is the framework of climate networks, and it offers a fundamentally different way of seeing the Earth system: not as a set of isolated variables, but as a web of coupled, interacting subsystems whose collective behavior gives rise to climate dynamics and extremes.

My research constructed and analyzed such networks from long-term observational and reanalysis data across the contiguous United States, uncovering structures invisible to conventional analysis — hub-periphery organization, modular clustering, long-range teleconnections, and gateway regions that mediate the propagation of atmospheric perturbations. These network-level properties shed new light on physical processes behind climate extremes including heat waves, air pollution, wildfires, and floods.

Correlation tells us that two variables move together — but it cannot tell us which one drives the other, or whether both are driven by a third. In a system as complex and nonlinear as the climate, distinguishing genuine physical causality from spurious association is one of the central methodological challenges. My research applied a range of causal inference frameworks — including Granger causality, convergent cross mapping (CCM), Peter-Clark momentary conditional independence (PCMCI), transfer entropy, and related information-theoretic approaches — to detect and quantify directed causal relationships within the climate system.

Rather than assuming linear cause-and-effect, these methods are suited to the nonlinear, high-dimensional, and often noisy dynamics of real climate data. Applied across the nodes of a climate network, they reveal asymmetric physical couplings: which regions causally pace extreme heatwaves across urban networks, which climate zones act as gateways for cross-regional precipitation, and how the directed influence of urban heat on air pollution — or vice versa — varies across cities and seasons.

The goal is always the same: to move from describing what the climate system does, to understanding what drives it.

A central and deeply motivating thread of my PhD was the question of tipping points and critical transitions — moments when a system that has been changing gradually crosses a threshold and shifts abruptly, often irreversibly, to a new state. Detecting these transitions before they occur is one of the hardest and most important challenges in Earth system science.

I explored this across multiple domains: climate phase transitions (cold-to-warm state shifts), ecohydrological vegetation regime shifts (abrupt greening or desertification), and critical hydroclimate transitions including sudden shifts in drought and precipitation dynamics. A key contribution was extending conventional statistics-based early warning signals — variance, autocorrelation — to system-level network analysis, showing that changes in network topology such as clustering coefficients, nodal density, and synchronization patterns precede critical transitions and serve as more sensitive and informative indicators.

The long-horizon vision that motivates this work is the prediction of tipping elements at global scale — the Amazon rainforest, the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation, Arctic sea ice, monsoon systems — whose crossing could trigger cascading transitions across the Earth system. Building data-driven, network-informed predictive frameworks for these elements remains a deeply challenging and deeply compelling open problem.

Advised by Prof. Morgan Levy at Scripps Institution of Oceanography, my postdoctoral research focuses on the physical drivers of extreme heat in California — a state whose geography produces some of the most striking regional contrasts in heat dynamics anywhere in the world. The interplay of factors shaping heat differs dramatically between the fog-cooled coast, the scorching Central Valley, and the sprawling, densely built urban corridors.

My research examines how multiple climatic and anthropogenic drivers interact and shift across California's diverse regions — from the fog-cooled coast to scorching inland valleys and densely built urban corridors. These include vegetation greenness and land–atmosphere coupling (through NDVI and surface energy fluxes), coastal low clouds and the marine layer (a critical but variable moderator of coastal temperatures), ecohydrological feedbacks, and anthropogenic land-use pressures. My tools are satellite remote sensing and machine learning — methods that allow me to unravel physical processes across California at the regional scale where they actually matter for people.
